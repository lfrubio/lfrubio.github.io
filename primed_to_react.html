Primed to (re)act

While researchers continue to study the effects of disproportionate minority contact with law enforcement on a range of outcomes, little work explores how these encounters lead to instances of mental, emotional, and/or physical trauma involving minority youth. From a human development perspective considered for adults and youth, we ask how everyday normal functioning (e.g., communication patterns) might potentially contribute to adverse encounters. In light of ongoing disproportionate minority contact, and the introduction of pro-active policing strategies likely to increase contact in minority-majority communities, trauma arising from negative youth-police interactions is an emergent public health concern for those most often impacted by these phenomena: male minority youth (MMY).

The long-term goals of this project are to (1) understand how the way officers and dispatchers describe incidents may impact the quality of police encounters with MMYs, and (2) develop an effective training program for law enforcement officers (LEOs) for reducing the incidence of these outcomes. A primary focus of this work will be a first of its kind computational strategy to the automated transcription of communications, reconstruction of policing incidents, and analysis of linguistic antecedents associated with lower or higher quality LEO-MMY encounters.

While working on this project, I:

  • Designed and collated a database of Federal Census, City departmental, and privately collected data based on behavioral and institutional mechanisms of Police-Citizen interaction
  • Designed a qualitative coding scheme and performed qualitative analysis of police-dispatcher communications
